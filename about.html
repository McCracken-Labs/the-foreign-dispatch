About

How this is made — and how to read it

The Foreign Desk exists to answer one question: what is the rest of the world actually saying about the United States? Not filtered through American commentary, but in the words of each country's own press. Every morning it gathers top US-related headlines from around the world, translates them into English, and links straight to the original article.

There are two feeds. World Press is the daily front page — mainstream and state outlets from a rotating set of countries. Independent collects nonprofit, reader-funded and investigative journalism, grouped by editorial orientation, for readers who want sharper, more critical analysis and a range of viewpoints.

How to read the leaning tags

No outlet is neutral, and pretending otherwise would defeat the purpose. On World Press, sources are tagged by their relationship to power:

Independent / neutral State-funded, broad editorial Pro-government State-controlled

Independent outlets are privately owned with editorial independence. State-funded, broad editorial (e.g. Al Jazeera, France 24) are publicly financed but carry a wide range of views. Pro-government outlets reliably reflect their government's line without being formally controlled. State-controlled outlets (e.g. RT, Global Times) are direct instruments of state messaging — still worth reading, precisely because they show what a government wants the world to believe.

The Independent page

Big outlets — even good ones — share blind spots. The Independent feed gathers the nonprofit newsrooms, reader-funded sites and investigative shops that tend to have more room for critical thinking. They are grouped by orientation — investigative, independent-left, libertarian, conservative, heterodox, academic — because reading across viewpoints beats marinating in one. Independent media leans left worldwide, so right-of-centre and heterodox voices are included deliberately for balance.

One group deserves special mention: reporting in exile. Where authoritarian governments make independent journalism dangerous, newsrooms often relocate abroad and keep covering home — including its dealings with Washington. Outlets like Meduza (Russia), IranWire (Iran), Hong Kong Free Press and The Kyiv Independent frequently carry US stories seen through the eyes of people the US most directly affects.

How sources rotate

To keep things fresh and give a genuinely global picture over time, the World Press homepage features a rotating slice of countries drawn from a larger pool. Whichever countries are central to the day's biggest US story are always included; the rest rotate. Every edition is preserved in the Archive.

An honest limitation

Some major Western outlets — the BBC, The Guardian, Le Monde, Der Spiegel, the Times of India — block the automated tool this site uses to read the web. Where possible their absence is filled with other credible outlets from the same region, but it does mean the accessible pool leans somewhat toward state-funded and Global-South media. This is disclosed rather than hidden.

A note on translation

Non-English headlines are translated to convey meaning and tone, not word-for-word. Where the original matters, it is shown beneath the translation. For anything consequential, follow the link and read the source.
